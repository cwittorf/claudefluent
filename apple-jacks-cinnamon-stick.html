Mascot History

Who Was Cinna-Mon?

If you grew up watching Saturday morning cartoons in the early 2000s, you remember Cinna-Mon — an animated orange cinnamon stick with attitude, sneakers, and a standing rivalry with his nemesis, Bad Apple.

The premise was deceptively simple: Kellogg's ran a series of Apple Jacks commercials where Cinna-Mon (the cinnamon stick) and Bad Apple (the green apple character) would race, compete, and scheme to prove which flavor dominated the cereal. Kids were invited to pick sides. It was the early-2000s version of a brand engagement campaign, and it worked brilliantly.

The Apple Jacks cinnamon stick mascot was charismatic in that particular 2000s cartoon way — a little smug, weirdly likable, and always two steps ahead of his apple-flavored rival. Cinna-Mon was orange. He moved fast. He won often. You were probably rooting for him.

Did You Know?

The "who wins?" campaign was interactive by design. Kellogg's encouraged kids to call a hotline or visit the website to vote for their side. Apple Jacks was running social engagement mechanics years before social media made that a standard playbook.

Mascot FAQ

Everything You Actually Searched.

What is the Apple Jacks cinnamon stick mascot's name?

The Apple Jacks cinnamon stick mascot's name is Cinna-Mon. He's an animated orange cinnamon stick character who appeared in Apple Jacks TV commercials starting in the early 2000s. His full name was styled with the hyphen — Cinna-Mon — which made sense given that he was literally a cinnamon stick with legs, attitude, and a rivalry with a green apple character named Bad Apple.

What happened to the Apple Jacks mascots?

Cinna-Mon and Bad Apple were quietly retired from Apple Jacks advertising sometime in the mid-2000s. They don't appear on current packaging or in current commercials. No official farewell was announced — they simply stopped showing up, as mascots often do when brand strategy shifts. The absence is probably why 27,000 people search for them every month.

Why did Apple Jacks change mascots?

Kellogg's never released a formal statement on the mascot retirement. The Cinna-Mon and Bad Apple campaign was very much a product of early-2000s cereal marketing culture — interactive, rivalry-driven, character-focused. As the 2000s progressed, cereal marketing broadly shifted away from dedicated mascot storylines toward lifestyle advertising and health-focused messaging. The characters didn't leave because they failed. They left because the era that created them moved on.

Why does Apple Jacks taste more like cinnamon than apple?

The cinnamon flavor in Apple Jacks is genuinely more pronounced than the apple flavor — and yes, that's probably by design. Cinnamon as a spice has a more aggressive, long-lasting taste profile than mild apple flavoring. The irony isn't lost on anyone, least of all Kellogg's, who literally built a marketing campaign around the debate. The Cinna-Mon vs Bad Apple rivalry was clever precisely because it was grounded in something real: the cereal does taste more like cinnamon.

Are Cinna-Mon and Bad Apple coming back?

There's no official announcement as of 2026. But 27,000 monthly searches for "apple jacks cinnamon stick" is the kind of search volume that brand teams notice. Never say never. The nostalgia market is real, the demand is documented, and Cinna-Mon had the charisma to sustain a comeback. Consider this an open letter.
